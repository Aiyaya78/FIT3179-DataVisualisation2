Outcome of GE15

The 15th General Election (GE15) resulted in Malaysia’s first-ever hung parliament, as no coalition secured the simple majority required to form a government.

Following the Yang di-Pertuan Agong’s advice to form a unity government, PH allied with BN, GPS, GRS, WARISAN, and several independents. PN declined to join and became the official opposition.

The GE15 outcome set the stage for a realignment of political power across Malaysia, influencing subsequent alliances in both Peninsular and East Malaysia.

Power Shift in the East Coast

The BN once held firm control over Malaysia’s east coast. In the GE15, the PN, led by the Malaysian Islamic Party (PAS), gained strong support across the east coast, particularly in Kelantan, Terengganu, and Kedah. PAS’s deep community networks helped PN end BN’s long-standing dominance in the region.



The Rise of East Malaysian Coalitions

In earlier elections, BN dominated East Malaysia through its local branches. By GE15, regional coalitions such as GPS, GRS, and WARISAN became the main political forces, replacing BN’s control. Their rise reflected East Malaysia’s stronger political influence and its key role in forming the unity government led by Anwar Ibrahim.

Turnout Patterns by Area

In the 2022 general election, 68 constituencies were classified as rural , 75 as semi-urban, and 79 as urban.

Voter turnout was slightly lower in rural areas, partly due to logistical challenges and greater distances to polling stations, while semi-urban areas showed more consistent participation. Both semi-urban and urban areas had at least one constituency reaching a turnout above 85%, reflecting highly engaged local electorates.

Across all area types, most constituencies recorded turnout rates between 75% and 80%, reflecting strong and stable voter engagement nationwide despite regional differences.

Election Context and Trends

Voter turnout peaked in GE13, reflecting strong public engagement but also leading to scrutiny over electoral irregularities and calls for reform.

In GE15, the voting age was lowered to 18 through the Undi18 policy, adding millions of new young voters. Yet turnout declined from GE14 as political instability and voter fatigue reduced enthusiasm.



Factors Behind Turnout Changes

Although election day was declared a public holiday, Malaysians could not travel to their registered hometowns to vote. Those working in other states or overseas often faced time and cost barriers.

Such challenges, combined with disillusionment among some voters, contributed to lower participation in several states despite the expanded electorate.

Even with the inclusion of Undi18, voters aged 21 to 60 remain the dominant group, as they represent the core of Malaysia’s working population and are more consistent in political participation.

The near-equal gender balance shows no major difference in voter representation, suggesting that both men and women are equally engaged in shaping national decisions.

Indigenous voters form the largest share of the electorate due to Malaysia’s demographic structure, giving them strong influence in determining political outcomes across most constituencies.
